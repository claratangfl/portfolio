Candidate Segmentation Framework for a Major Job Platform

Lifecycle segmentation · 283 candidates, 3 segments · BTL/ATL marketing strategy

Context

A major job platform wanted a segmentation framework that could drive relevant, timely communication through a candidate's job search. Its existing segments described who was active, but not what to do about it. Two candidates with identical activity could be in completely different mindsets: one browsing idly at lunch, the other applying to everything in sight. Send them the same email and one message is always wrong.

I designed a lifecycle segmentation framework with six stages, mapped each stage to specific marketing actions below and above the line, set out the technical requirements to keep it running, and tested it against a sample of 283 candidates. Conversion ranged from 12.0% to 16.4% across segments, and distinct timing and channel patterns showed when and how each group should be reached.

1. The Problem

The platform already had candidate segments, but they described activity levels rather than intent. The brief asked for a framework that could drive "relevant and timely communications through the job search process." That is a lifecycle prompt: the segmentation needs to tell Marketing what action to take, when, and through which channel.

I evaluated three possible segmentation axes:

  • Demographic (industry, seniority): tells Marketing who someone is, but not what to do
  • Value-based (engagement score): ranks candidates, but does not map to actions
  • Lifecycle stage: tells Marketing what to do, because each stage implies a different message, channel, and frequency

The test: what would change in the email a candidate receives depending on which segment they are in? If the answer is "not much," the segmentation is not actionable enough.

2. The Framework

Six lifecycle stages, defined by measurable behavioural signals. The framework is cyclical: candidates move backwards as well as forwards. Someone dormant can re-engage and jump to Warming Up. A new candidate who applies on day one skips straight to Actively Applying.

Lifecycle stages and behavioural signals
StageDefinitionKey Signal
NewProfile created <14 days, <5 job views, no applicationsAccount creation
PassiveActive in last 30 days, <3 visits/month, <5% view-to-apply ratioLow frequency browsing
Warming UpVisit frequency doubled over prior 30 days, OR profile/resume updatedAcceleration in activity
Actively Applying3+ applications in 14 days, weekly visitsHigh intent, high volume
Slowing DownWas Actively Applying within 30 days, activity dropped 50%+Deceleration from peak
DormantNo activity for 30+ days after a previously active stageAbsence of signal
Design principle: Stages evaluate highest-intent first. If a candidate qualifies for multiple stages, they are assigned to the highest. This keeps the framework mutually exclusive without multi-label complexity.

One refinement worth making in version two: Dormant covers two meaningfully different states. A candidate pausing their search needs a different win-back approach to one who disengaged because they found a job. Splitting these, or at least tagging the likely cause from prior-stage behaviour, would sharpen the re-engagement strategy.

Enrichment attributes

The lifecycle stage tells Marketing what to say. Enrichment attributes tell them how to reach the candidate: industry preference, channel preference, peak activity time, profile completeness, location, and career level. Each attribute has a reliability rating, because Marketing should not personalise by industry if a candidate has only viewed 3 jobs.

Key insight: Frequency and tone change with each stage. A "Warming Up" candidate who gets passive weekly digests is being under-served. A "Slowing Down" candidate who gets daily emails is being annoyed. Matching communication intensity to lifecycle stage is how the platform builds the perception of relevance.

Above the line: stage distribution informs brand strategy

Segment distribution tells Marketing what the brand priority should be. If the largest segment is Dormant, the priority is re-engagement. If Warming Up is growing fast, there is a market moment to capitalise on. Segment profiles also build sharper media briefs: "find more people who look like our Actively Applying segment" is far more precise than "target job seekers aged 25 to 45."

4. What the Sample Data Revealed

I analysed 283 candidates across two months and three existing segments. The sample is small (patterns are directional, not conclusive), but three findings stood out.

Conversion varies by segment

View-to-application conversion rate by segment

Segment 2 converts at 16.4%, Segment 3 at 12.0%. Segment 3's gap persists across channels, so it is behavioural, not a channel problem. A lifecycle framework would diagnose whether they are passive browsers or application drop-offs. Segment 1 is excluded from this comparison: its average of 26.9 applications per applier masked a median of just 6.5, so a handful of power-appliers distort its headline rate.

Timing patterns reveal candidate context

Activity by hour of day, share of segment activity

Curves are smoothed for illustration. The anchored data points are the observed peaks: Segment 1 at 1pm, Segment 2 at 5pm (11.8% of all its activity in that single hour), Segment 3 at 7pm.

Segment 1 peaks at 1pm (desktop browsing during work hours). Segment 2 spikes at 5pm (post-shift healthcare and education workers). Segment 3 peaks at 7pm (evening mobile). This maps directly to send-time optimisation.

Channel preference is industry-linked

Channel dominance by segment type

Indicative channel mix. Observed signals from the data: Segment 1 is desktop-dominant, Segment 2 is the most channel-diverse, Segment 3 is iOS-dominant with the lowest multi-channel usage (31%).

Key finding: ICT and Engineering candidates are desktop-dominant. Trades and Manufacturing are mobile-dominant. Campaign channel strategy should follow industry targeting, not segment labels. Segment 3 converts at roughly 12% regardless of channel, confirming the lower conversion is a behaviour pattern, not a channel friction issue.

5. Technical Requirements

Four things need to be true for the framework to work operationally:

  1. Daily recalculation. Lifecycle stages change fast. Weekly refresh risks sending a re-engagement email to someone who applied to five jobs yesterday.
  2. Segment tags reach all execution systems. Email, onsite personalisation, ad platforms. If the tag lives in a warehouse but cannot get to the ESP, it is just a report, not a tool.
  3. Quarterly threshold review. If 80% of candidates end up in one stage, the thresholds need tuning. This is expected drift, not failure.
  4. Segment distribution dashboard. Both the early warning system and how you prove value to leadership over time.

What breaks over time

  • Thresholds go stale: behaviour shifts over months. Fix: quarterly review triggered by distribution skew alerts.
  • Upstream data changes silently: a product team changes event logging and segments shift overnight. Fix: automated week-over-week distribution monitoring.
  • No one owns the definitions: within a year, Marketing, Product, and Analytics use different versions of "Active." Fix: single owner with a version-controlled definition document.

6. If This Is Week 1 on the Job

  1. Validate lifecycle stages against the full dataset. Does the distribution look reasonable, or does everything cluster in one stage?
  2. Build the segment distribution dashboard. The ongoing proof point and early warning system.
  3. Test one communication hypothesis. Start with Dormant win-back: easiest to measure, clear baseline, clear outcome.
  4. Sit down with the campaigns team. Ask what data they actually need. The enrichment attributes I proposed are my best guess from the outside. They will know what is missing.

7. Methodology

Analysis worked in layers: event-level rates first, then candidate-level summary tables, then median vs average comparisons (which revealed that Segment 1's average of 26.9 applications per applier masked a median of just 6.5, driven by a few power-appliers). Final layer: cutting by channel, timing, and industry to check whether segment differences held or were explained by confounding variables.

  • 283 candidates, 2 months of data. Patterns are directional, not conclusive.
  • Channel and timing patterns are the most reliable findings at this sample size (consistent across the data, not driven by outliers).
  • Conversion rate differences (16.4% vs 12.0%) would need chi-squared testing on a full dataset to confirm.
  • Stage thresholds (14 days, 5 views, 3 visits per month) are starting points drawn from the sample distribution and domain knowledge. On the job, they would be validated against the platform's existing internal definitions and recalibrated quarterly against actual distribution data.
