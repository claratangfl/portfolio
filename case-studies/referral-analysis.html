Refer a Friend: What the Data Tells Us About Referral Quality

Neobank referral program · Jan/Feb 2021 cohort, five years on · 6,393 referrals

Context

A neobank ran a refer-a-friend programme and doubled the sign-up bonus from $5 in January to $10 in February. My task was to work out whether the larger bonus actually brought in better customers. I analysed 6,393 referrals across both cohorts, following each one from sign-up through to whether they were still active five years later.

Doubling the bonus tripled volume but attracted customers far less likely to stay. February's cost per active customer ($74) is 2.4 times January's ($31), and each extra customer the higher bonus drew in cost $116 to acquire. Bonus size, though, matters less than two other factors: card activation (50% retention vs 3.5%) and inviter quality (50% vs 21%) are what actually drive whether a customer stays, with the bonus cohort a distant third.

1. The $10 Bonus Worked Too Well

When the bonus doubled in February, daily referrals jumped from about 55 to 168. But new customers were less likely to engage and stay at every step. The gap widens at each milestone, reaching 16 percentage points by long-term retention.

Customer journey funnel: Jan ($5) vs Feb ($10)
Key finding: February's higher bonus brought 2.75x the volume but the quality gap compounds at every milestone, from 9 points at funding down to 16 points at retention.

2. More Money Brought More Ghosts

One in five February referrals disappeared within a week. I split the waste into two groups:

  • Ghosts: no real activity within 7 days. February had 750 (16%), up from 183 (11%) in January.
  • Bonus gamers: made 5 quick purchases to collect the bonus, then left within 45 days. February had 127 (2.7%), up from 13 (0.8%) in January.
Waste breakdown: ghosts and gamers by cohort

Together, these two groups cost $18,810, which is 17% of February's total spend going to people who were never going to stay. January's waste rate was 8%.

4. What Actually Predicts Whether Someone Stays?

I ranked every factor in the data by how much it affects 5-year retention. Card activation is by far the strongest factor. Inviter quality is second. The bonus cohort difference is third and less than half the size of card activation.

Retention impact by factor (percentage point difference)

These factors stack. Customers who activated their card, funded their account, had an active inviter, and came from a single-referral inviter retain at 65.4%. The average is 36.2%.

5. The Card Makes the Difference

Customers who activated their physical card: 50% still active. Those who never activated: 3.5%. There is no gradual decline. It drops off a cliff.

To check whether activating the card is a cause or just a signal of engagement, I controlled for prior behaviour. Even among highly engaged customers (funded, purchased, made 5+ purchases), the gap holds.

Retention rate: card activated vs not, across engagement levels
Key finding: Even among customers who made 5+ purchases, those without a card are only 18% active vs 58% with one. The card turns an app into a wallet habit. 75% of activators do it within 30 days, but timing does not matter: whether someone activated on day 8 or day 200, retention is about 50%.

6. Inviter Quality Drives Everything

More referrals per inviter means lower retention

Inviters who refer 5+ people produce customers with 16% retention, compared to 43% for single-referral inviters. They are sharing links widely rather than making genuine recommendations.

Retention by referrals per inviter

Inviter quality is a two-step chain

Inviter quality drives card activation, and card activation drives retention. The best inviters (active, single referral, 6+ months tenure) produce 84% card activation and 55% retention. The least engaged inviters produce 52% card activation and 17% retention.

7. What the Program Could Look Like

I modelled what would happen if we had restricted who could refer in February. Just requiring an active inviter cuts cost per active customer by 27%. Adding a referral cap brings it further down.

8. Recommendations

Do first

  1. Only let active, tenured customers refer. Require the inviter to be active and have been on the platform for at least 3 months. This cuts cost per active customer by 27%.
  2. Cap referrals at 1-2 per inviter. People who refer 5+ others produce customers with 16% retention. A cap corrects volume-over-quality incentives without hurting genuine word-of-mouth.

Schedule

  1. Push card activation in the first 30 days. Reminders for people who have funded but not activated could convert the group most likely to respond.
  2. Test the bonus amount properly. Run an A/B test: $5 with restrictions vs $10 without.

Worth investigating

  • Revenue per customer: is "still active" actually a good measure of value?
  • Referral chains: do good customers become good inviters?
  • Card delivery speed: faster delivery might close the gap between sign-up and activation.
  • Funded but no card: ~1,000 customers put money in but never activated. A targeted prompt could convert them.

9. Methodology and Caveats

  • This is two months of data, not an experiment. The January vs February difference could partly reflect seasonality.
  • No revenue data. "Still active" (transacted within 30 days of data extract) is a proxy.
  • 7 duplicate rows were removed (6,393 unique referrals from 6,400 rows).
  • Inviter identity is approximate, grouped by a combination of inviter attributes.
  • The card activation finding is a strong correlation, not a proven cause.
  • Geography and generation were explored but mostly set aside: 93% of referrals are within the same state (too little variation to learn from), the Jan vs Feb quality gap held across all states, and only same-generation referral showed a measurable effect (+4.0 points).
