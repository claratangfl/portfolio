Measuring Subscription Growth From Imperfect Data

D2C pet food · 10,000 active cat subscriptions · Q4 2021 growth model

Context

A D2C pet food company needed to measure quarterly growth in its active cat subscriptions. The data made that harder than it sounds: six source tables at different grains (customer versus cat), no column recording current subscription status, ambiguous pause events, and orphaned records.

I assessed data quality across every table, designed a scalable data model to resolve the grain mismatches, calculated Q4 2021 growth, and traced the main churn drivers. The base grew 11.0% quarter on quarter. Product fit accounted for 48% of voluntary cancellations, which is where any retention work should start.

1. The Data Was Not Ready to Analyse

Before any growth calculation, I ran a systematic data quality assessment across all six source tables. This surfaced issues that would have silently broken any downstream metrics.

Key decision: Paused cats are counted as not active for growth metrics. A cat paused with no unpaused_date at quarter end is counted as paused, not active. This is consistent with the subscription model but must be stated explicitly because the data does not make it obvious.

2. Data Model Design

I designed a medallion architecture (bronze, silver, gold) to handle the grain mismatches and enable scalable growth reporting.

Architecture layers and core entities
LayerPurposeKey Tables
BronzeRaw ingested tables, no transformations, audit trail preservedRaw sources (6 tables)
SilverStaged, cleaned, deduplicated, timezone-normalisedstg_customers, stg_cats, stg_events
GoldAnalytical mart: fact + dimension tables, pre-aggregated growth martfact_subscription_events, dim_customer, dim_cat, dim_reason, mart_growth_daily

The core design decision: a fact_subscription_events table with one row per state-change event per cat (SCD Type 2 snapshot pattern). Columns: cat_id, from_status, to_status, event_timestamp, reason_id. This resolves the grain mismatch by converting customer-level events to cat-level events during the silver-to-gold transformation.

From model to data product

The model feeds a growth dashboard built for the Growth team's operating rhythm. Must-haves:

  • KPI row: current active cats and net growth, shown as counts, dollars, and percentage change
  • Waterfall chart: opening → acquisitions → reactivations → resumptions → pauses → cancellations → closing
  • Cohort retention grid: percentage of acquired cats still active by month since acquisition
  • Reason breakdowns: top cancel and pause reasons as ranked bars
  • Side filters: product and customer segment toggles, so different stakeholders can self-serve their own cut
  • Drill-through table: individual cat-level events for operational use

4. What Is Driving Churn?

Pause reasons: mostly involuntary

Pause reason breakdown (Q4 2021, 846 events)

52.8% of pauses are "Did not pay order" (reason_id 1), flagged as involuntary. These must be separated before any voluntary churn analysis is meaningful. They point to payment workflow barriers that need investigation. Within the voluntary remainder, over half of recorded reasons land in "Other", so the usable signal is limited until the taxonomy improves.

Cancel reasons: product fit is the primary driver

Cancel reason breakdown (voluntary cancellations)

Product fit combines "cat didn't like food", "gone off food", and "tummy trouble transitioning".

Key finding: 48% of voluntary cancellations relate to product fit: "cat didn't like food," "gone off food," and "tummy trouble transitioning." Interventions should focus on flavour variety, trial packs, and transition support. However, 27% of cancel reasons and 55% of pause reasons land in "Other" or "Unknown," meaning signal is lost at collection. Improving the taxonomy is a prerequisite for reliable retention analysis.

5. Recommendations

Improve data before improving retention

  • Fix the reasons taxonomy. With 27% of cancels and 55% of pauses as "Other," you cannot diagnose churn drivers reliably. Reduce these categories before running retention interventions.
  • Add subscription status at the cat grain. Define and align on Active/Inactive definitions across teams. Currently, status must be inferred from event sequences.
  • Implement snapshot data. Eliminates double-counting of intra-period cyclers (cats that pause and resume within the same quarter).

Address product fit churn

  • 48% of voluntary cancels are product fit issues. Test flavour variety packs and structured transition support for new cats.
  • Run cohort analysis by acquisition channel to identify whether low-fit customers are being attracted by specific campaigns.

Recover paused subscriptions

  • 60.8% of Q4 pauses are still paused at December 31. Target these with structured reactivation campaigns, especially those with known reasons (freezer space, food preference).
  • Investigate the payment workflow for the 52.8% of non-payment pauses. Understand barriers and assess the need for a payment recovery programme.

Extend the analysis

  • Pause-to-cancel conversion rate: what % of paused cats eventually cancel?
  • Time-to-cancel from first subscription (early churn vs mature churn)
  • Average subscription revenue per cat to size the commercial impact
  • NPS/CSAT data correlated with pause and cancel behaviour

6. Methodology and Assumptions

  • One active subscription assumed per cat (not per customer)
  • Pause and cancel events within Q4 are assumed to originate from the opening active pool of 10,000
  • A "resume" is inferred as: pause_end IS NOT NULL AND subsequent status = active
  • A "reactivation" is inferred as: a new active period following a cancel event
  • Non-payment pauses are included in growth metrics (the cat is still not active) but excluded from voluntary churn analysis
  • Referential integrity check: 494 orphaned cat records (2.4%) excluded from analysis
  • Deduplication applied using ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY cat_id, event_type ORDER BY event_timestamp)
